Missing Data — The Hidden Problem

Interactive: visualize what missing data looks like in a real dataset

In Geldium's dataset, key fields like income, payment history, and credit utilization had missing values. This is extremely common in financial data. Below, each square represents a data record. Red = missing, purple = complete.

⚠️
Why this matters for AI

If the model trains on incomplete data, its predictions will be skewed. A model might incorrectly classify a customer as "low risk" simply because their income data is missing — not because they're actually safe.

Three Types of Missingness

MCAR
Missing Completely At Random

The missing-ness has no pattern. Like random data corruption. Safe to delete or impute.

MAR
Missing At Random

Missing-ness depends on another variable. Example: high-income customers less likely to report salary.

MNAR
Missing Not At Random

The fact it's missing IS the information. Customers in financial distress may hide their debt levels.

🔧

How to Fix Missing Data — Imputation Strategies

What Geldium used and why each method was chosen
✅
Used for: Missing income values

Replace missing values with the median (middle value) of the available data. Unlike mean, the median is resistant to extreme outliers — perfect for income data where a few billionaires would skew the average.

Example: If incomes are [30K, 35K, 40K, 45K, 200K] and one is missing, the mean is 70K (inflated by 200K). The median is 40K — far more representative.

📊
Used for: Missing credit utilization records

Uses a statistical model to predict the missing value based on other available columns. Since credit utilization correlates with debt balance and income, those can be used to estimate it.

Why preferred: Credit utilization is a critical risk factor. Simply deleting those records or using an average would lose important predictive signal. Regression preserves the relationship between variables.

⚗️
Used for: Incomplete payment history records

Generate artificial data that statistically resembles real data, without exposing real customer records. Useful when a large chunk of behavioral data is missing and deletion would hurt the dataset too much.

Caution: Synthetic data must be carefully validated. If it doesn't match real-world distributions, it can introduce bias and create unrealistic patterns that mislead the model.

🗑️
Last resort: Remove rows with missing data

Only appropriate when a very small percentage of records (typically <5%) have missing values AND the missingness is completely random (MCAR). Otherwise, deletion biases the remaining dataset.

Risk: If you delete all records where income is missing, and those tend to be low-income customers, your model will never learn about that customer segment — a form of selection bias.

PRIMARY MODEL — Logistic Regression

Selected as Geldium's main delinquency prediction model.

Logistic regression predicts the probability that something belongs to one of two categories. In this case: delinquent (1) or non-delinquent (0). It outputs a number between 0 and 1.

P(delinquent) = 0.87 → HIGH RISK → trigger intervention
P(delinquent) = 0.12 → LOW RISK → monitor only
Interpretability
High

Every coefficient tells you exactly how much a feature increases risk.

Regulatory fit
Excellent

Regulators can audit and explain every decision made.

Accuracy
Good

Strong on structured financial data. Not as powerful as neural networks on complex patterns.

Deployment
Simple

Lightweight, fast to run at scale on millions of customers.

🌳
SUPPORT MODEL — Decision Trees

Used alongside logistic regression to improve explainability.

A decision tree asks a series of yes/no questions about a customer to classify them. Think of it like a flowchart that a business analyst could actually draw on a whiteboard.

Example decision path:

Has missed ≥2 payments? YES
  → Credit utilization >80%? YES
    → CLASSIFY: HIGH RISK

Has missed ≥2 payments? YES
  → Credit utilization >80%? NO
    → CLASSIFY: MEDIUM RISK
Interpretability
Very High

Visual, easy for non-technical stakeholders to understand.

Overfitting risk
High

Can memorize training data and fail on new customers.

NOT CHOSEN — Neural Networks

Too complex and opaque for regulated financial decisions.

Neural networks are inspired by the human brain. They use interconnected layers of nodes to detect extremely complex patterns. Very powerful — but come at a cost.

How Do You Know If Your Model Is Good?

The 6 evaluation metrics used for Geldium's delinquency model

You can't just use "accuracy" — that's dangerously misleading. If 95% of customers are non-delinquent, a model that always says "not delinquent" has 95% accuracy but is completely useless. Here's what actually matters:

Accuracy
Overall correct predictions

Of all predictions, how many were right?

Precision
Of flagged cases, how many are real?

Reduces false alarms. Important for customer trust.

Recall ★
Of real risks, how many caught?

Most critical! Missing a true risk = financial loss.

F1 Score
Balance of precision + recall

Harmonic mean — only high when both are good.

ROC-AUC
How well model separates classes

1.0 = perfect. 0.5 = random guessing.

🎯
Why Recall is the most important metric here

In delinquency prediction, a false negative (missing a risky customer) is far more costly than a false positive (flagging a safe customer). A missed delinquent costs the bank real money. A false alarm just means an unnecessary outreach call. Maximize recall.

End-to-End Modeling Workflow

The 8-step process used to build Geldium's delinquency model
Step 01

Collect & Preprocess Data

Gather all customer financial records. Clean duplicates, standardize formats, handle encoding inconsistencies. The foundation of everything else.

Step 02

Handle Missing Values

Apply median imputation for income, regression imputation for credit utilization, synthetic generation for missing payment history records.

Step 03

Feature Selection

Choose the most predictive variables: payment history, credit utilization, DTI ratio, outstanding balance, recent credit activity. Remove redundant or biased features.

Step 04

Train / Test Split

Split the dataset (typically 80% training, 20% testing). The model never sees test data during training — this ensures honest evaluation of real-world performance.

Step 05

Train the Model

Feed the logistic regression model historical customer behavior. It learns the statistical relationship between each feature and delinquency outcomes.

Step 06

Generate Probability Scores

The trained model outputs a risk score (0–1) for every customer. Scores above a defined threshold (e.g., 0.65) are classified as high-risk.

Step 07

Evaluate Performance

Calculate accuracy, precision, recall, F1, ROC-AUC. Analyze the confusion matrix to understand where errors cluster. Prioritize recall over precision.

Step 08

Fairness & Bias Audit

Review predictions across demographic groups. Check for proxy bias (e.g., ZIP code correlating with race). Apply SHAP to explain individual decisions.

The SMART Recommendation Framework

How to turn a vague AI insight into a specific business action

Executives don't want to hear "the model found some patterns." They want to know: what do we do, by when, and what's the expected outcome? SMART recommendations answer all of that.

Geldium's Actual SMART Recommendation

"Launch a 6-week proactive SMS and email outreach program targeting customers with high credit utilization and multiple missed payments, with the goal of reducing 30+ day delinquency rates by 10%."

S

Specific

SMS + email outreach. Not just "contact customers" — exactly which channel, targeting exactly which segment (high utilization + missed payments).

M

Measurable

Reduce 30+ day delinquency by 10%. A concrete number you can track before and after.

A

Actionable

SMS and email campaigns are something the collections team can realistically execute with existing infrastructure.

R

Relevant

Directly tied to Geldium's core business objective: reduce financial losses from delinquencies.

T

Time-Bound

6 weeks. A defined window creates urgency and allows for proper before/after comparison.

The Four-Layer Architecture

How data flows through Geldium's autonomous collections system
📥

Layer 1 · Data Pipeline

Continuously ingests customer data: payment history, credit utilization, transaction behavior, real-time repayment activity. Raw data → structured features. This layer never stops running — new data arrives every hour, updating risk scores in real-time.

Payment historyCredit utilizationTransaction behaviorReal-time activity
🧠

Layer 2 · Decision Engine

The brain. Uses the trained logistic regression model + business rules to assign each customer a risk tier (Low / Medium / High / Critical) and select the appropriate intervention strategy. The engine considers both the model's probability score and business context (e.g., don't contact customers who've requested no contact).

Risk scoringCustomer segmentationIntervention selection
📤

Layer 3 · Action Layer

Executes the decision automatically. Low risk → no action. Medium risk → automated SMS reminder. High risk → email with payment assistance offer. Critical risk → escalate to human agent. Actions are personalized: timing, channel, and message vary by customer preference and behavior history.

SMS remindersEmail offersPayment plansAgent escalation

Layer 4 · Learning Loop

This is what makes it agentic. After every intervention, the system observes the outcome: Did the customer make a payment? Did they respond to the SMS? Did the payment plan work? These outcomes feed back into the model, improving future decisions. The system gets smarter every week.

Human-in-the-Loop: Why AI Can't Do It All

Where human judgment is non-negotiable

Even the most sophisticated AI system must have humans in the loop for certain decisions. This isn't a limitation — it's a feature of responsible design.

AI Handles Autonomously

  • Scoring millions of customers in real-time
  • Sending automated reminders and notifications
  • Prioritizing collections agent queues
  • Generating risk reports and dashboards
  • Routine payment plan offers
  • Monitoring repayment behavior

Humans Must Decide

  • Customer disputes about their account
  • Escalated hardship cases
  • Decisions with legal implications
  • Fairness and bias reviews
  • Regulatory compliance sign-off
  • Sensitive customer situations
⚖️
The principle: AI amplifies humans, not replaces them

A collections agent who previously managed 50 accounts manually can now manage 500 high-complexity cases — because the AI handles the 10,000 routine ones. Human expertise is applied where it matters most.

Responsible AI

Ethics, Fairness & Regulations

An AI model that's accurate but unfair is not a success. Responsible AI requires deliberate design choices and ongoing monitoring.

Three Types of AI Bias in Financial Services

Understanding how discrimination creeps into models — even unintentionally
Bias Type
How It Enters + Real Example
Historical Bias

Pattern inherited from past

If historical loan decisions unfairly denied credit to certain communities, the AI learns "these groups → high risk" — not because they're risky, but because past decisions were discriminatory. The model perpetuates old injustice.

Selection Bias

Unrepresentative training data

If Geldium's historical data skews toward urban customers, the model may perform poorly on rural customers it has little data about — not because they're riskier, but because they're underrepresented in training.

Proxy Bias

Indirect demographic signals

ZIP code seems like a neutral financial feature. But ZIP code correlates strongly with race and income due to historical housing segregation. Using ZIP code as a feature can lead to indirect racial discrimination — even if race is never in the dataset.

The Four Regulations Governing Geldium's AI

Compliance isn't optional — these laws define the boundaries of what the AI can and cannot do
ECOA
Equal Credit Opportunity Act

Prohibits discrimination in credit decisions based on race, gender, religion, national origin, or age. Any AI model must prove its decisions are not correlated with protected characteristics.

GDPR
General Data Protection Regulation

EU regulation requiring: data minimization, right to explanation, customer consent. If a customer asks "why was I denied?", the AI must be able to explain in plain terms. Automated decisions require human oversight.

FCA
Financial Conduct Authority

UK regulator requiring fair treatment of customers. Collections practices must be proportionate, non-harassing, and consider vulnerable customers. AI must have overrides for edge cases.

FCRA
Fair Credit Reporting Act

Governs how credit information is collected, used, and shared. Customers have the right to dispute inaccurate information. AI systems using credit data must follow strict protocols for data accuracy.
